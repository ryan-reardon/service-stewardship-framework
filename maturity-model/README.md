# Maturity Model

The Service Stewardship Framework includes a four-level maturity model to evaluate and guide the progression of technology service ownership. The purpose of the maturity model is not to score for compliance. It is to create clarity, surface structural risk, and enable intentional improvement.

---

## Why a Maturity Model Exists

Technology services rarely fail because of a single incident. They fail because structural weaknesses accumulate across:

- Strategy
- Reliability
- Architecture
- Economics
- Governance
- Organizational capability

The maturity model provides a structured way to:

- Diagnose current state honestly
- Identify systemic weaknesses
- Prioritize improvement efforts
- Track measurable progression over time

It transforms stewardship from reactive management into deliberate leadership.

---

## The Four Levels of Maturity

Each domain within the framework progresses through four levels:

---

### Level 1: Reactive

- Processes are informal or undocumented.
- Risk is discovered through failure.
- Ownership depends on individuals rather than systems.
- Metrics are incomplete or lagging.

Characteristics:
- Firefighting dominates.
- Technical debt is unmanaged.
- Governance is inconsistent.

---

### Level 2: Managed

- Core processes are documented.
- Basic governance cadence exists.
- Key metrics are tracked.
- Responsibilities are defined.

Characteristics:
- Reduced chaos.
- Improved visibility.
- Early-stage predictability.

---

### Level 3: Optimized

- Metrics drive decision-making.
- Risk is proactively monitored.
- Trade-offs are intentional and transparent.
- Automation reduces operational friction.

Characteristics:
- Structured investment allocation.
- Reduced surprise.
- Measurable reliability and cost control.

---

### Level 4: Proactive

- Predictive signals inform strategy.
- Risk is addressed before failure.
- Continuous improvement is embedded.
- Cross-domain alignment is intentional and systemic.

Characteristics:
- Strategic clarity.
- Economic discipline.
- Sustainable service evolution.

---

## Scoring Philosophy

Maturity scoring must be:

- Evidence-based
- Conservative
- Domain-specific
- Reviewed periodically

Avoid aspirational scoring.

A domain should only be considered at a given level if:

- Required artifacts exist
- Governance cadence is active
- Metrics are consistently measured
- Evidence can be demonstrated

See:
- [Scoring Guidance](scoring-guidance.md)

---

## Domain-Level Maturity

Each of the 12 domains is evaluated independently.

This enables:

- Targeted improvement
- Focused investment
- Risk-based prioritization

Service maturity is not an average score.

A single Level 1 domain in Security or Reliability can create enterprise risk regardless of higher scores elsewhere.

---

## Using the Maturity Model

Recommended cadence:

- Baseline assessment upon adoption
- Quarterly review of priority domains
- Annual full reassessment

Suggested workflow:

1. Complete the consolidated assessment.
2. Identify lowest maturity domains.
3. Conduct deep-dive review using domain modules.
4. Define improvement plan.
5. Track movement across maturity levels.

Full operational guidance:
- [How to Use the Framework](../docs/how-to-use.md)

---

## Progression Principles

Improvement should be staged.

Do not attempt to move directly from Level 1 to Level 4.

Each maturity transition should correspond to:

- Increased visibility
- Reduced risk exposure
- Stronger governance
- Clearer ownership
- Measurable operational stability

Progression must be sustainable.

---

## The Objective

The goal of the maturity model is not perfection. It is predictability, durability, and intentional stewardship. Services do not drift upward in maturity. They drift downward without discipline. This model exists to prevent that.
