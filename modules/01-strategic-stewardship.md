# Strategic Stewardship

Strategic Stewardship ensures the service remains aligned to enterprise strategy and delivers measurable value throughout its lifecycle.

It is the discipline of continuously validating:

- Why the service exists?
- Who it serves?
- Is its investment profile justified? 
- How should itevolve over time?

Strategic Stewardship transforms ownership from operational maintenance into intentional leadership.

---

# 1. Domain Purpose

To ensure that:

- The service has explicit strategic intent.
- Investment is proportional to value delivered.
- Lifecycle stage drives governance expectations.
- Modernization is proactive, not crisis-driven.
- Strategic recalibration is embedded into operating rhythm.

Without Strategic Stewardship, services drift into reactive backlog management and unmanaged complexity.

---

# 2. Target State (Level 3–4 Characteristics)

A strategically mature service demonstrates:

- A 3–5 year rolling roadmap aligned explicitly to enterprise objectives
- Clearly defined customer segments and validated use cases
- Quantified business value drivers (revenue enablement, cost avoidance, risk reduction, productivity gains)
- Explicit lifecycle stage classification (Growth, Scale, Sustain, Sunset)
- Dedicated modernization capacity protected from reactive erosion
- Annual strategic recalibration embedded into governance
- Transparent cost-to-value modeling
- Proactive retirement or consolidation decisions

At higher maturity levels:

- Investment decisions are informed by demand forecasting
- Scenario modeling influences roadmap planning
- Adoption and satisfaction trends are forecasted and acted upon early
- Lifecycle transitions are anticipated rather than reactive

---

# 3. Core Responsibilities

Strategic Stewardship requires the Engineering Manager or Service Owner to:

- Translate enterprise strategy into actionable engineering priorities
- Maintain a defensible, capacity-aligned roadmap
- Ensure every major initiative has a measurable outcome definition
- Balance reliability, modernization, feature development, and cost control
- Maintain technical debt visibility and structured remediation planning
- Continuously reassess service relevance and competitive posture
- Escalate strategic misalignment early

Ownership is measured by clarity of direction, not volume of delivery.

---

# 4. Governance Model & Cadence

Strategic Stewardship operates on a defined cadence.

**Weekly**
- Review roadmap execution and emerging risk indicators

**Monthly**
- Review adoption metrics and financial posture
- Validate capacity allocation across run/grow/modernize categories

**Quarterly**
- Reassess roadmap alignment to enterprise strategy
- Re-evaluate lifecycle stage classification
- Review modernization allocation effectiveness

**Annually**
- Conduct structured lifecycle review
- Challenge continued relevance of the service
- Approve major investment, repositioning, or sunset decisions

Governance requires a defined cadence to be effective.

---

# 5. Required Artifacts

The following artifacts must exist and remain current:

- 3–5 year rolling roadmap (minimum 12-quarter directional view)
- Documented value realization model
- Customer and use-case inventory
- Lifecycle classification statement
- Technical debt register
- Capacity allocation model (run vs grow vs modernize)
- Strategic risk log
- Financial posture summary (TCO and cost-to-value trend)

Artifacts should be actively used in decision-making, not archived.

---

# 6. Operating Mechanisms

Strategic Stewardship relies on structured mechanisms:

- Roadmap scoring rubric tied to measurable business outcomes
- Initiative intake governance process
- Explicit modernization capacity guardrail
- Sunset trigger policy for low-value or obsolete services
- Quarterly strategic narrative shared with stakeholders
- Capacity alignment checkpoint before accepting new commitments

These mechanisms create investment discipline.

---

# 7. Key Metrics

Strategic Stewardship must be measurable.

Representative metrics include:

- Adoption growth rate
- Customer satisfaction trend
- Roadmap execution percentage
- Capacity allocation (run/grow/modernize distribution)
- Cost-to-value ratio
- Technical debt trend
- Forecast vs actual delivery variance
- Investment mix distribution

Metrics must inform trade-offs and resource allocation decisions.

---

# 8. Leading Risk Indicators

Watch for:

- Roadmap driven by the loudest stakeholder
- No quantified outcomes tied to initiatives
- Modernization perpetually deferred
- Services never retired or consolidated
- Capacity overcommitment without trade-off clarity
- Strategic discussions replaced by reactive firefighting
- Financial posture reviewed only during budget season

Strategic erosion is gradual and often invisible until impact accumulates.

---

# 9. Maturity Progression Model

Strategic Stewardship evolves through staged progression:

**Level 1: Reactive**
- Named service owner identified
- 12-month roadmap documented
- Primary customers and use cases defined

**Level 2: Managed**
- Initiative-level outcomes quantified
- Lifecycle stage classification introduced
- Adoption and satisfaction trends tracked
- Modernization allocation rule established

**Level 3: Optimized**
- Scenario-based investment modeling implemented
- Capacity allocation model formalized
- Quarterly strategic review summary published
- Cost-to-value ratio trends tracked

**Level 4: Predictive**
- Demand forecasting integrated into roadmap planning
- Strategic dashboards automated
- Early-warning indicators for lifecycle transitions established
- Portfolio-level alignment continuously reviewed

Progression should be intentional and evidence-based.

---

# 10. Relationship to Other Domains

Strategic Stewardship sets direction for:

- Organizational Capability (team structure must support strategy)
- Value Execution (delivery must align to measurable outcomes)
- Financial Stewardship (investment must remain justified)
- Innovation & Modernization (evolution must be proactive)
- Portfolio Alignment (service must fit enterprise ecosystem)

Without Strategic Stewardship, other domains optimize locally without strategic coherence.

---

# Summary

Strategic Stewardship defines whether a service endures or erodes. Operational stability without strategic clarity is temporary. Durable services are the result of disciplined direction, proportional investment, and structured lifecycle governance.
